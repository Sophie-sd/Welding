Introduction: The Crucible of Structural Integrity

When aluminium assemblies must perform under sustained load, the choice between TIG (GTAW) and MIG (GMAW) is not merely a preference — it is a metallurgical decision with direct consequences for joint strength, corrosion resistance, and service life.

At KHODAK Metal Solution, we evaluate each project against material grade, thickness, access constraints, and aesthetic requirements before specifying a welding process.

Technical Breakdown

The following comparison summarises key parameters our engineering team considers during specification review.

Parameter TIG (GTAW) MIG (GMAW)
Precision & Control Excellent — ideal for thin sections and critical root passes with independent amperage and foot-pedal control. Good — faster deposition but less fine control on heat input for thin aluminium.
Speed & Throughput Slower — suited to high-value, low-volume precision work. Faster — preferred for production runs and thicker plate assemblies.
Material Thickness 0.5mm – 6mm aluminium with superior cosmetic finish. 3mm+ aluminium where speed outweighs cosmetic requirements.
Aesthetic Finish Superior — uniform bead profile, minimal spatter, no slag. Acceptable — may require post-weld cleanup on visible joints.

Case Study Snapshot: High-Stress Aluminum Truss

A marine facility required an aluminium truss assembly exposed to salt-spray conditions. TIG welding with 5356 filler was specified to maintain corrosion resistance at every joint. Full penetration was verified by dye penetrant inspection.

Industrial Best Practices

Regardless of process selection, these protocols are non-negotiable on every KHODAK project.

⚙

Surface Preparation

Remove oxide layer within 30 minutes of welding. Use stainless steel wire brush dedicated to aluminium only.

Gas Selection

100% argon for TIG up to 6mm. Argon-helium blends for thicker sections requiring increased heat input.
